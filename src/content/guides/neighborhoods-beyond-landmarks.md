A neighborhood video is usually strong evidence of **atmosphere** and weak evidence of **access**. The camera may cross a courtyard threshold, cut out a long walk, hide stairs, or show a resident who never agreed to become a destination. “Go here and wander” transfers that uncertainty to the traveler.

A useful neighborhood guide builds a small public route:

**public start → two or three named anchors → seated or staffed pause → reliable exit**

It also states what remains out of scope. The product should never turn laundry, an open front door, a child, a mahjong table, or a matchmaking notice into a pin.

## Resolve the video before drawing the route

Classify every extracted object:

| Video evidence | Map treatment | Why |
|---|---|---|
| “Beijing hutongs” location tag | City-level idea only | Too broad to route or verify access |
| Named public lane such as `五道营胡同` | Route anchor | The street identity is resolvable |
| Museum, park, café, shop, or metro station | Exact place candidate | Verify Chinese name, branch, entrance, and hours |
| Unnamed doorway, courtyard, longtang gate, or shortcut | Do not pin | Public access and identity are unknown |
| Resident, exercise group, market vendor, or personal notice | Context only | A person is not a destination or reusable media asset |

Keep the evidence record practical:

```text
Source and date:
Public place claim:
English / official Chinese identity:
City, district, entrance, or street number:
Public access evidence:
Surface, steps, toilet, seating, shade, and traffic notes:
Start / pause / exit role:
Opening or reservation checked:
Resolution — resolved / probable / unresolved:
```

If the video shows a “hidden lane” but supplies no readable street sign, storefront, or city context, it remains an unresolved scene. Do not attach it to the nearest famous hutong.

## Beijing: learn the courtyard form without entering a home

### Public interpretation route

Start at **Shijia Hutong Museum / 史家胡同博物馆**, a public museum at No. 24 Shijia Hutong that Beijing describes as a space for interpreting hutong history and resident memory. Check its current opening and reservation instructions before leaving; city listings have shown limited daytime hours and Monday closure.

The museum is the right place to study courtyard layouts, household objects, and neighborhood change. It removes the false choice between understanding domestic architecture and peering into a private courtyard.

After the museum, keep to public streets and choose one staffed café, bookstore, or transit point as the exit. Do not use “open courtyard” as a discovery filter. Doorways may serve multiple homes, deliveries, toilets, bicycles, or businesses even when they look picturesque.

### Wudaoying–Chengxian public-lane walk

**Wudaoying Hutong / 五道营胡同** is a named public lane with shops and cafés. Beijing's official route material describes it as a 632-metre lane and pairs it with **Chengxian Street / 成贤街**, the historic street by the Confucian Temple and Imperial College.

Use Wudaoying as the public start and Chengxian Street as the second anchor. Decide before arrival whether the ticketed temple complex is part of the plan; a street walk and attraction visit are different products. End at a metro entrance or a verified Didi pickup point rather than assuming every hutong mouth is a good vehicle stop.

This is a commercial-historic walk, not an “untouched local secret.” That honesty helps: cafés can provide seating and toilets, while narrow curbs, deliveries, parked bikes, and crowds still need to be checked on the day.

### Bell Tower route

For a compact old-city loop, use **Beijing Drum and Bell Towers / 北京钟鼓楼** as the public landmark. Beijing has published a roughly 1.3-kilometre route through named surrounding lanes, including Zhongku, Lingdang, Caochang North, Zhonglouwan, Doufuchi, and Yuge hutongs.

The surrounding street route is public; climbing either tower is a separate ticketed attraction with opening and last-entry times. Save the tower entrance, not just the district label `钟鼓楼`. If steps or height are unsuitable, keep the square and street loop and omit tower entry.

## Shanghai: give the famous photo route two exits

Shanghai's official citywalk starts at **Jiaotong University Metro Station / 交通大学站**, passes **Wukang Building / 武康大楼** and **Anfu Road / 安福路**, and ends at **Changshu Road Metro Station / 常熟路站**. The published version can take four to six hours because it includes many shops and former residences.

Build the shorter version around the public street rather than every viral storefront:

1. Start at Jiaotong University station and confirm the exit that minimizes road crossings.
2. View Wukang Building from the public pavement without blocking the junction or photographing through occupied windows.
3. Continue along a chosen Wukang–Anfu segment, saving only shops or museums you genuinely plan to enter.
4. Exit at Changshu Road station, or set a named staffed venue as the pickup point if fatigue, heat, rain, or crowds shorten the walk.

Wukang–Anfu is now a major shopping and social-media destination; Shanghai's own material describes heavy footfall and frequent commercial openings. Do not sell it as an undiscovered residential neighborhood. Its value is a concentrated public architecture-and-retail walk with good exit options.

For a seated green pause, **Fuxing Park / 复兴公园** is a separate exact destination at 105 Yandang Road. Shanghai's official listing currently gives daily hours and free admission, but verify again for the trip date. Park activity varies by weather, season, enforcement, and who happens to be there; no guide can promise a choir, dance, tai chi group, or matchmaking gathering.

## Chengdu: community street versus park experience

GoChengdu's visitor guide identifies **Yulin North Road Community / 玉林北路社区** as a place for public street life, bookstores, cafés, art, and food. Save that as a neighborhood anchor, then resolve the actual bookstore, café, market, or restaurant separately. “Yulin” alone can refer to a broad area and should not be presented as one pin.

Use public commercial streets and a named staffed stop. Do not follow a video into an apartment compound, residential stairwell, community room, mahjong space, or market back area unless it is clearly open as a business or public event. Set the return pickup at a main road or verified venue rather than an internal lane.

**Chengdu People's Park / 成都人民公园** is a different experience. It is a public park containing paths, teahouses, services, and changing social activity. The park pin does not resolve Heming Tea House, an ear-cleaning provider, a performance, or a matchmaking area; each claim needs its own identity and current context.

Treat matchmaking notices as personal information, not cultural décor. Do not photograph or transcribe age, income, property, phone, or family details. Watching from a respectful distance does not require turning a real family's search into content.

## Wet markets are businesses, not “groceries as theater”

Do not send a traveler to “any wet market.” Resolve the exact market, official Chinese name, public entrance, operating period, and whether photography is permitted. A generic map search can land at a wholesale facility, staff entrance, relocated market, or residential produce shop.

Inside a public market:

- keep aisles, scales, payment points, and scooter paths clear;
- ask before photographing a person, stall display, price board, preparation area, or live animal;
- do not handle food or equipment without invitation;
- buying something is optional and does not purchase consent to film;
- confirm weight, unit, and total before paying;
- leave if staff say no, signage prohibits photography, or the space is too busy to stand safely.

A useful contribution records access, entrance, hours checked, surface, crowding, payment method, and whether photography was allowed. “Felt authentic” is not execution evidence.

## Park activity is not an open workshop

Morning and evening activity varies enormously. A park can contain exercise groups, choirs, dancing, games, water calligraphy, family gatherings, or none of them. Weather, holidays, local rules, and group routines matter more than a national “6–10am” promise.

Stay outside the activity footprint. Watch without blocking paths or seats. Join only after a clear invitation from an organizer or participant; being waved around an obstruction is not an invitation. Ask before recording identifiable faces or audio, and do not assume a public performance permits commercial reuse.

If the experience depends on a particular activity, treat it as unconfirmed until a current local source or organizer verifies the time. Keep the park itself as the pin and the activity as a dated claim.

## Build the route for the least mobile moment

Historic streets can look flat on a map while remaining difficult in practice. Curbs, uneven paving, bollards, parked scooters, narrow passages, tower stairs, rain, heat, toilets, and scarce seating can change a short route.

Before leaving:

1. Keep the route to two or three public anchors in one district.
2. Check weather, daylight, attraction hours, and the exact station exits.
3. Verify a step-free entrance and toilet directly if either is essential; do not infer them from a general accessibility icon.
4. Add a seated staffed pause with an address you can show.
5. Save the hotel card and a Chinese Didi pickup point on a main road.
6. Share the route and expected return time when walking alone.
7. Turn back at the time limit even if the social feed suggests one more “hidden” stop.

The goal is not to get lost. It is to notice a city while preserving the ability to leave—and without making someone else's front door part of the itinerary.
